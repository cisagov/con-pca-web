Instructions for reading this report

The CON-PCA Phishing Assessments are organized into 90-day campaigns in which each of your users will be phished once every 90 days from the start of your subscription.

Each of these individuals here after referred to as targets are tracked on 4 potential actions.

  1. Opening the email
  2. Clicked a malicious link
  3. Submitted data on malicious site, or
  4. Reported the email

To track statistics on the reported email, that information must be supplied to DHS in the required format or a reporting mailbox must be available and advertised in the consuming organization.

Most organizations will setup a reporting mailbox that employees can forward suspicious emails to for further investigation. In order to determine some statics such as time to first report, the email content, the target email address, and time of first report will be required to determine those statics.

This report provides the results for the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) National Cybersecurity Assessments and Technical Services- (Vulnerability Management) led Continuous Phishing Campaign Assessment (CON-PCA) for {{ detail?.customer.full_name }}. The CON-PCA is a practical exercise intended to support and measure the effectiveness of security awareness training for information system users. The results of this CON-PCA show the susceptibility of {{detail?.customer.identifier}} personnel to social engineering attacks—specifically email phishing attacks—in which an adversary tricks an email user into clicking a malicious link to gain unauthorized network access. In the scenario that this CON-PCA tested, the phishing attack bypassed technical controls that would normally detect and/or block malicious emails and links. The CON-PCA, therefore, measured {{detail?.customer.full_name}}’s level of vulnerability to a successful phishing attack by targeted user click rates, click times, response rates, and response times, as shown in Table 1.

This report aims to enhance {{ detail?.customer.full_name }}'s understanding of their information system users’ cybersecurity behavior and to promote a more secure and resilient workforce.

Click Rate vs. Report Rate

One of the most common measures of an organization’s susceptibility to phishing attacks is targeted-user click rates. If an actual phishing attack is able to bypass technical controls and arrive in a user’s inbox, the human target must agree to click on the malicious link or attachment for the attack to be successful. In practical exercises such as this PCA, targeted-user click rates will fluctuate depending on the email complexity used in testing. Effective security awareness training, however, should result in a noticeable click rate decrease over time and to a level deemed acceptable based on the organization’s risk management posture.

The counterpart to user click rates is the user-reporting rate, determined by the number of emails sent or calls to alert {{ detail?.customer.full_name }}’s helpdesk during each campaign. Based on previous testing, VM recommends that organizations aim to have two people reporting the phishing attempts for every person that clicks. This ratio ensures that there is not only reporting coverage for the person clicking, but also redundant coverage in case the person who clicks the link does not report or does not realize they have been phished. Effective security awareness training should result in a noticeable report rate increase over time and to a level deemed acceptable based on the organization’s risk management posture.

In this report, the percentages shown by the user click rate and user report rate represent, respectively, the percentage of targeted people (determined by a unique email address) who have clicked at least once on a “malicious” link and those who reported a suspicious email. The number of unique clicks correlates to the number of end user devices potentially compromised in each campaign. The number of user reports correlates to the number of opportunities the {{ detail?.customer.full_name }} security team had to identify a potential breach and reduce its impact. Click Time vs. Report Time

To contain an attack, an organization’s security team must be aware of the potential breach. In the scenario that this PCA tested, no technical controls were triggered during a phishing attack and the clock for a potential breach started once a targeted user clicked on a “malicious” link. Timely user reporting decreases the window of opportunity that an adversary has to access data or gain further network entry. Timely reporting also increases the opportunity the security team has to detect and respond to a potential breach. By educating users on how to both spot and promptly respond to phishing attempts, an organization can improve their anti-phishing defenses.

Appendix C: Templates

The phishing templates used throughout this service include various levels of complexity. Each level is based on a calculation of the factors designed to entice users to click on a malicious link. These factors are:

  1. Appearance and Grammar: Appearance can be professional or poor. Poorly written emails should be easier to identify as a phish or compromise attempt.
  2. Link Domain: Is the link deceptive in its appearance, such that it appears similar to an actual or real domain but is not the real address. Is the link spoofed or does the link appear hidden to hide the intended domain.
  3. Logo / Graphics: Does the phishing email utilize logos or graphics stolen from a legitimate organization to present itself as legitimate.
  4. Sender External: Is the sender external to the organization. External senders are more likely to be a phish.
  5. Sender Internal: Does the sender appear to be internal, but actually come from an external source.
  6. Authoritative: Does the email appeal to an authority or have an authoritative tone? Appealing to an
  7. Relevancy Organization: Does the email utilize information that is relevant or timely to the organization specifically.
  8. Public News: Does the email utilize public news or current events to appear legitimate or hide links or other deceptions.
  9. Utilizing Fear: Does the email utilize fear to appeal to immediate action.
  10. Appealing to Duty or Obligation: Does the phishing attempt will appeal to a general sense of duty or obligation. Targeted emails may have identified a specific job responsibility of the target and appeal to that responsibility to motivate an action.
  11. Curiosity: Does the phishing attempt setup a situation of a curious or interesting but unresolved nature that can only be determined by clicking the link?
  12. Greed: Does the phishing attempt offer free items, deeply discounted items, job offers, or other appeals to an individual’s greed.

Low: A low level template are those which should be easy to identify as a phishing email. The email may be poorly written. The link domain may be blatantly unrelated, come from an external sender, or have outlandish and ridiculous claims.

Moderate: Moderate level templates are more sophisticated then low level and should be more difficult to detect. This level of email may have well written prose, be grammatically correct, and professional in display but be general in nature.

High: High level phish templates typically target specific individuals or groups within an organization and utilize information that generally would not be know by the public at large. They use this targeted information to provide a feeling of familiarity to better deceive the target. This level is the most difficult to identify as malicious.
